Teaching Philosophy

First-year writers are learning new genres, new conventions, new standards for what "college writing" looks like. They are navigating a new institution, often managing anxiety about whether they are doing things "right." In my writing center work, I learned that writers develop through revision and feedback — through multiple drafts, through conversation, through practice in low-stakes spaces. Online, I look for a different way to create that iterative space.

★

This belief shapes the tools I create. Writing is a process, not a product. Students improve through iteration, through receiving feedback, through trying again. Rather than assigning lectures about annotated bibliographies or citation format, I wanted to build spaces where students could practice those forms themselves — where the guidance is embedded in the doing, and where they are forced to engage in critical thinking and analysis underneath.

★

Citation Station is straightforward: enter your source information, generate a correct citation. The Annotated Bibliography tool does something different. It slows students down with sentence-level prompts that require critical thinking and analysis. "What is this source's main argument?" "How does this challenge your thinking?" The form itself becomes a vehicle for critical thought.

★

The tools meet students where they already are — on their devices, in their own time. Low-stakes practice allows students to slow down, try again, and refine their thinking without the weight of a grade. In digital writing spaces, the process becomes visible.
