Course Outcomes for This Assignment

  • Generating Inquiry. Students will be able to generate and explore genuine lines of inquiry related to writing, language, literacy, and/or rhetoric.
  • Multiple Ways of Writing. Students will be able to purposefully integrate multimodality, multiple languages, and/or multiliteracies into writing products to support their goals.

Background

The first several weeks of class have focused on linguistic diversity, the myth of standardized English, and the many ways we use language to communicate effectively with different audiences. We've addressed common myths about writing — for example, that good grammar equals good writing. Most of our conversations have centered on how we do language and why we should recognize the value in our different language practices.

"There are no gaps or limits when it comes to languages; there are only gifts." — Sara P. Alvarez

Sara P. Alvarez, Amy J. Wan, and Eunjeong Lee write that we are "language architects" — we make critical decisions about how to negotiate and use language successfully in response to specific situations. Our social and cultural contexts inform how we use language, and social and cultural realities create norms and biases that influence how we understand it, which is why it's important to evaluate social and cultural power. Language is tied to identity.

"I am my language. Until I can take pride in my language, I cannot take pride in myself." — Gloria Anzaldúa

The Assignment

You're going to identify and analyze a cultural artifact. Select an object or symbol and provide context for it — explain how it connects to specific traditions, beliefs, and values within your community. We all have different backgrounds that shape how we see ourselves and the world. This assignment is asking you to reflect on your identity and culture, to evaluate something that has helped shape you, and to share that knowledge with us.

Writing is social and collaborative. Through peer review and the nature of this assignment, we'll gain new knowledge and learn about and from each other.

Some possibilities to get you thinking:

Cast iron skillet cornbread
A religious practice
Tailgating or cookouts
Knitting or crocheting
A piece of jewelry
A diary or scrapbook
A childhood stuffed animal
Baseball cards or Barbies
Coins or memorabilia
A family tradition
A piece of music
Something of your own

Choose only one thing.

Two Ways In

This assignment allows you to celebrate something meaningful to you. But you could also approach it through a more critical lens — investigating an object or symbol that doesn't hold as much value to you now, or something you find problematic about your culture or community. Maybe you want to examine your own previous understandings, ask hard questions, and share how your thinking has changed over time.

Either way, you're going to have to research. You're being asked to move beyond your own personal experience and perspective — through interviews and other sources.

A Note on Language

Since we've been talking about the dynamic nature of language, I encourage you to write this in your own voice, dialect, and language. You might choose to switch back and forth between languages — mixing more formal "academic" English with your own dialect. There's a lot of space for you to be creative. I hope you take that up.

Expectations

Meets (Baseline)

  • 4 full pages, double-spaced, Times New Roman 12pt
  • MLA format (header, margins, in-text citations, Works Cited)
  • One source from class readings
  • Two additional sources — one of which may be an interview

Grading Rubric

Total: 30 points  (15% of course grade  |  200-point scale)

Category Exceeds — 8–9 pts Meets — 6–7 pts Approaching — 4–5 pts Does Not Meet — 0–3 pts
What the Paper Is About
9 pts  /  30%
Does the paper have a clear focus and say something meaningful about the artifact?
 The paper has a clear, specific focus from the start. The writer explains what the artifact is, why it matters, and what they want the reader to understand. The main point is purposeful and holds together throughout. The paper has a clear focus. The reader can tell what artifact the writer chose and why it matters to them. The main point comes through, even if it's not always stated directly. The paper has a focus, but it's vague or shifts around. The reader has a general sense of the artifact but isn't sure what the writer is trying to say about it. It's hard to tell what the paper is about or why the artifact matters. There isn't a clear main idea.
Analysis & Depth
9 pts  /  30%
Does the paper go beyond description and dig into what the artifact means?
 The writer goes well beyond describing the artifact — they explain what it means, ask interesting questions, and connect it to bigger ideas about culture, identity, or community. The analysis feels genuine and thoughtful. The writer does more than just describe the artifact. They make connections between the artifact and their community, culture, or identity and offer some explanation of why those connections matter. The paper mostly describes the artifact without fully analyzing it. Some connections are made, but they aren't developed or explained in depth. The paper describes the artifact but doesn't analyze it. There are no real connections to culture, community, or identity.
Using Sources
1.5 pts  /  5%
Does the paper include one class reading and two other sources (one may be an interview)?
 All three sources are present and woven into the paper naturally. It's clear how each one connects to the writer's ideas.  (1.5 pts) All three required sources are present and cited. Sources are connected to the paper's ideas, even if not always smoothly.  (1 pt) Sources are included but feel dropped in without explanation, or one required source is missing.  (0.5 pts) Fewer than three sources are used, no class reading is included, or sources aren't connected to the paper's ideas.  (0 pts)
Voice, Identity & Language
9 pts  /  30%
Does the paper sound like a real person and reflect honestly on identity and experience?
 The paper has a strong, distinctive voice. The writer reflects honestly and specifically on their identity and what the artifact means to them. Language choices — including dialect or code-switching if used — feel intentional and effective. The paper has a consistent voice and the writer reflects meaningfully on their own experience and identity. The writing feels personal, not generic. There are moments of personal voice, but the writing sometimes feels generic or distant. The reflection on identity or experience stays on the surface. The paper has little personal voice or reflection. It reads as detached from the writer's actual experience or identity.
Format & MLA
1.5 pts  /  5%
Does the paper meet the basic formatting requirements?
 4+ full pages, double-spaced, Times New Roman 12pt, correct MLA header. In-text citations and Works Cited are accurate with very few errors.  (1.5 pts) Meets the 4-page requirement and follows MLA formatting with only minor errors. Works Cited is included.  (1 pt) Close to but doesn't fully meet the length requirement, or has several MLA formatting errors. Works Cited has mistakes.  (0.5 pts) Significantly shorter than 4 pages, MLA formatting is largely missing, or no Works Cited page.  (0 pts)
